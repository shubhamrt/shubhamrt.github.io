01 About

Landscapes as living networks of connection

Drylands cover about 40% of Earth's land surface and support billions of people. When they degrade, the consequences ripple outward: dust storms, lost livelihoods, entire ecosystems shifting into new states.

"Degradation doesn't happen uniformly. It flows through the landscape along pathways of water, wind, and vegetation."

During my PhD at Durham University — as an Early Stage Researcher in the Marie Skłodowska-Curie i-CONN network — I developed a network-based approach to understanding dryland degradation. The key insight: landscapes aren't collections of isolated components. They're living systems of connections, and those connections determine resilience.

Now, as a Senior Hydrologist at the UK's Environment Agency and postdoctoral researcher at New Mexico State University and the Jornada Experimental Range, I'm extending this thinking to wind-driven processes and dust emission dynamics across the western United States.

Different erosive forces — water, wind — same underlying question: what makes a landscape resilient, and what tips it over the edge?
