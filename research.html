Research

Connectivity, Degradation
and Landscape Resilience

Understanding landscapes as
connected systems

My core research interest is dryland connectivity and land degradation. I study how water, wind, sediment, and vegetation interact across landscapes to drive degradation or sustain resilience, and how we can detect critical transitions before they become irreversible. The central framework I apply is network science: treating landscapes as graphs, where patches of vegetation or bare soil are nodes and the flow of resources between them are edges. The structure of this network determines how disturbance propagates.

Beyond the dryland focus, I am broadly interested in applying network theory to environmental systems more generally, including rainfall pattern reconstruction, aquatic habitat connectivity, and early-warning signals in complex systems. I also work in operational hydrology at the UK Environment Agency, where I apply these ideas to real-world catchment management, flood response, and drought monitoring.

Current and recent research themes:

  • Dryland connectivity and land degradation network-based approach to understanding grass-shrub regime shifts, hillslope erosion, and connectivity thresholds
  • Dust source dynamics and aeolian processes mapping wind erosion source areas across the western USA using remote sensing and field instrumentation
  • Water-driven erosion and vegetation patterning UAV/LiDAR surveys quantifying how vegetation spatial structure controls runoff and sediment flux
  • Rainfall network analysis graph-theoretic approaches to rainfall data reconstruction and pattern analysis, applied across the Murray-Darling Basin and the UK
  • Multilayer networks in hydrology theoretical development of network frameworks for coupled environmental systems
  • Operational hydrology flood frequency analysis, hydrograph separation, drought monitoring, and regulatory permitting

Field Work & Key Concepts

Arizona, USA, Dec 2022

Sonoran Desert Rangeland

Resources flow pathways, wind sediment transport, and grass-shrub patterning in a semi-arid rangeland. This site provided key field evidence for the network-based approach to hillslope connectivity.

image3.png
Key Concept

Islands of Fertility

Shrubs act as resource accumulation points, creating fertile islands surrounded by bare, depleted inter-patch soil. A hallmark of degraded dryland systems and a key indicator used in my connectivity framework.

image4.png
Key Concept

Length of Connected Pathways

A network metric that quantifies the extent over which bare soil patches are hydrologically linked, used as a proxy for erosion risk and system instability. Longer connected pathways signal higher vulnerability to degradation.

image5.png
Key Concept

Relative Productivity Index

A remote-sensing indicator derived from NDVI that quantifies vegetation productivity relative to local potential, used to track degradation trajectory across drylands and evaluate restoration outcomes.

image6.png
Key Concept

Sediment Connectivity and Erosion

When connectivity between bare patches exceeds a critical threshold, sediment transport shifts from localised to system-spanning, triggering accelerated erosion. Identifying this threshold is central to early-warning detection.

Operational Hydrology
