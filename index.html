I’m a computational geomorphologist and ecohydrologist working at the interface of applied science and environmental management. My work uses network analysis and numerical modeling to quantify hydrologic and sediment connectivity—translating complex system dynamics into actionable guidance for land and water management.

What I study

My research examines patterns of connectivity in ecogeomorphic systems, with an emphasis on understanding land degradation processes in drylands. I build network-based representations of landscape processes to identify key pathways, thresholds, and stability signals.

Key Concepts

Four ideas that organize most of my work.

Connectivity How water, sediment, and resources move—and what controls their pathways.
Networks Representing landscapes as nodes and links to measure structure and change.
Stability & thresholds Detecting early signals of shifts (e.g., grass–shrub transitions) and regime change.
Decision-ready metrics Simple outputs that support monitoring, restoration, and adaptive management.
