resilient drylands

Hey there, I'm Shubham.

I'm fascinated by a question that drives most of my research:

How do dryland landscapes fall apart and how can we see it coming before
it's too late?

Right now, I'm a postdoctoral researcher at New Mexico State University and Jornada Experimental Range, studying dust source dynamics across the western United States. Currently, I'm a Senior Hydrologist at the UK's Environment Agency. I completed my PhD at Durham University as an Early Stage Researcher in the Marie Sk≈Çodowska-Curie i-CONN network, developing a network-based approach to dryland degradation. I learned to see landscapes as living systems of connections rather than isolated components.

Drylands cover about 40% of Earth's land surface and support billions of people. When they degrade, the consequences ripple outward: dust storms, lost livelihoods, entire ecosystems shifting into new states. But degradation doesn't happen uniformly. It flows through the landscape along pathways of water, wind and vegetation. If we can map these pathways, we can identify where systems are vulnerable and intervene before collapse.

My journey has taken me from grasslands in the southwestern USA to floodplains in Austria, from analyzing rainfall networks in Australia's Murray-Darling Basin to flying drones over semi-arid hillslopes in Arizona. Along the way, I became one of the youngest Senior Hydrologists in the Environment Agency.

What excites me most is bridging science and real-world action. During my PhD, I focused on water-driven erosion and how vegetation patterns control landscape stability. Now I'm extending that thinking to wind-driven processes and dust emission. Different erosive forces, same underlying question: What makes a landscape resilient and what tips it over the edge?

Thanks for stopping by. Feel free to explore my research or reach out if you want to chat about drylands, networks or anything in between.
