Connectivity that guides
resilient drylands

Hey there, I'm Shubham.

I'm fascinated by a question that drives most of my research: How do dryland landscapes fall apart and
how can we see it coming before it's too late?

Right now, I'm a postdoctoral researcher at New Mexico State University and Jornada Experimental Range, studying dust source dynamics across the western United States. Currently, I'm a Senior Hydrologist at the UK's Environment Agency. I completed my PhD at Durham University as an Early Stage Researcher in the Marie Skłodowska-Curie i-CONN network, developing a network-based approach to dryland degradation. I learned to see landscapes as living systems of connections rather than isolated components.

Drylands cover about 40% of Earth's land surface and support billions of people. When they degrade, the consequences ripple outward: dust storms, lost livelihoods, entire ecosystems shifting into new states. But degradation doesn't happen uniformly. It flows through the landscape along pathways of water, wind and vegetation. If we can map these pathways, we can identify where systems are vulnerable and intervene before collapse.

My journey has taken me from grasslands in the southwestern USA to floodplains in Austria, from analyzing rainfall networks in Australia's Murray-Darling Basin to flying drones over semi-arid hillslopes in Arizona. Along the way, I became one of the youngest Senior Hydrologists in the Environment Agency.

What excites me most is bridging science and real-world action. During my PhD, I focused on water-driven erosion and how vegetation patterns control landscape stability. Now I'm extending that thinking to wind-driven processes and dust emission. Different erosive forces, same underlying question: What makes a landscape resilient and what tips it over the edge?

Thanks for stopping by. Feel free to explore my research or reach out if you want to chat about drylands, networks or anything in between.

Research

I use connectivity thinking (often via complex networks) to understand how dryland systems organize, how they destabilize, and how we can detect vulnerability early. My work spans dryland degradation, dust emission risk, spatiotemporal rainfall networks, and broader “connectivity science” across environmental systems.

Dryland connectivity & degradation

Network-based metrics to quantify hydrological and sediment connectivity, identify critical nodes/pathways, and link spatial vegetation–water–soil interactions to system stability and regime shifts.

  • Hydrological & sediment connectivity across grassland/shrubland hillslopes
  • Connectivity metrics as early-warning indicators of change
  • Management-facing framing: from theory to action (e.g., grazing/connectivity)

Dust source dynamics & aeolian processes

Extending connectivity ideas to wind-driven pathways: where landscapes become erodible, how dust “turns on,” and how process-based models and network diagnostics can improve dust emission risk prediction.

  • Dust source erodibility and spatial patterning across the western US
  • Connectivity pathways in aeolian transport networks
  • Geospatial + modeling workflows for large monitoring datasets

Rainfall networks & spatiotemporal hydroclimate

Network methods for rainfall reconstruction and sensor-network design, plus analysis of spatiotemporal rainfall organization and its implications for hydrology and geomorphic response.

  • Rain-gauge network “node importance” vs resolution & density
  • Network-based daily rainfall reconstruction (Murray–Darling)
  • Spatiotemporal rainfall change analysis (ongoing work)

Interdisciplinary connectivity science

Connectivity ideas travel well: I’ve worked on multilayer networks in landscape ecology and broader dynamical-network questions, including collaborations outside geomorphology/hydrology.

  • Multilayer networks in landscape ecology (habitat connectivity)
  • Functional connectivity & dynamical processes in networks
  • Collaborative work across environmental + computational domains

Key Concept

A few ideas that show up repeatedly in my work, explained in plain language.

Connectivity

Not just “who is next to whom,” but how water, sediment, wind, and resources actually move through a landscape. Connectivity is about pathways, bottlenecks, and leverage points.

Critical nodes & pathways

Some places matter disproportionately. Network metrics can reveal “critical nodes” where intervention may have outsized effects (or where failure cascades).

Thresholds & early warning

Dryland degradation often involves thresholds. The goal is to detect shifts before they lock in, using signals from patterns of connectivity and variability.
