Research focus

I'm interested in why landscapes tip — how grasslands become shrublands, how soils get exposed and eroded, and how small patches of bare ground connect into large, persistent scars. I use network theory to map and quantify these connections, turning messy field data and drone surveys into actionable insight for land managers.

⛰

Connectivity & erosion

I model how water and sediment move downslope in patchy drylands. By treating every bare patch, shrub, and micro‑channel as nodes in a network, we can identify critical nodes where intervention has the biggest payoff.

  • Grass → shrub regime shifts
  • Aridity & grazing pressure
  • Wind-driven sediment feedbacks
Based on PhD work in UK/US drylands.
🐄

Grazing & restoration tools

My goal is decision support. I translate connectivity maps into where to rest paddocks, add barriers, or re‑seed vegetation to slow runoff and hold soil in place. This links fundamental geomorphology to day‑to‑day land management.

  • Adaptive grazing strategies
  • Targeted restoration zones
  • Monitoring landscape resilience over time
Emerging work under review in applied ecology.
🤖

Drones, lidar & ML

I work with high‑resolution topography (UAV, terrestrial LiDAR), rainfall networks, and machine‑learning workflows. That includes QA/QC pipelines, reproducible analysis, and open tools in Python for hydrology teams.

  • UK CAA‑certified drone pilot
  • Hydrological modelling (HEC‑RAS, SWAT, etc.)
  • Python, MATLAB, GIS, Google Earth Engine
Also supporting flood + drought risk work in the UK.
